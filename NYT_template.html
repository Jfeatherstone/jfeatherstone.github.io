How Well Do We Understand Lethal Injection?

According to a new study, the US lethal injection procedure lacks basic medical justification.

By Jack Featherstone

  • March 3, 2021

The Coronavirus pandemic has brought immense attention to the efforts put forth to understand and mitigate the side effects of prescription drugs, vaccines, and other medications. Apart from this pursuit of health and longevity, a very similar effort exists in a much more morbid context: execution by lethal injection. With a successful dosage resulting in death, you might ask what side effects could possibly be worth considering, but even this macabre prescription needs to be thoroughly understood, as demonstrated by a recent study.

In this article, Teresa Zimmers and her colleagues investigate the currently accepted lethal injection procedure, including its origins, rationale, and efficacy. They present several results on the subject, including a statistical analysis of recorded executions, and a qualitative study of individual cases. Their results paint a dismal picture of the state of lethal injection, highlighting the lack of research that has gone into both the procedure, and its implementation.

Two of the main foci of the article are that the conception of the lethal injection process lacked scientific rigor, and that there has been little effort to monitor executions. One would expect at the very least that the chemicals used would be carefully chosen based on empirical evidence, right?

According to a Human Rights Watch investigation cited in the article, this is not the case at all. The medical examiner who first defined the procedure for use in Oklahoma, which other states later accepted without further investigation, “had no expertise in pharmacology or anesthesia and [they] did no research to develop any expertise.” This medical examiner simply selected three chemicals that should carry out the execution.

The three chemicals in question are thiopental (anesthetizes and causes respiratory arrest), potassium chloride (causes cardiac arrest), and pancuronium bromide (paralyzes and causes respiratory arrest and circulatory depression). The authors explain how the numerous modes of death complicate any determinations of how a patient actually dies, as well as whether they are adequately anesthetized throughout the process. While the chemicals can be lethal in isolation, their combined lethality hasn’t been previously studied.

Although the procedure has been in practice since 1984, and undergone slight changes to doses throughout that period, critical evaluations of the process only began in 2007. While previous works have hinted that the lethal dosage may need to be adjusted, this study illuminates numerous issues that beset the very foundation of the whole procedure. Thiopental and potassium chloride are shown to not reliably induce respiratory and cardiac arrest in the expected time interval, while the former also isn’t as effective of an anesthetic as it was originally thought to be.

Furthermore, the study highlights how not only the drugs themselves, but also their administration, is flawed. The practitioners who carry out human executions are not required to be proficient in any medical field, and the selection of such individuals is usually left to the discretion of the prisons.

On the other hand, administrators of potassium chloride to rats used in research experiments, the very same chemical used for humans, must “be trained and knowledgeable in anesthetic techniques, and be competent in assessing the level of unconsciousness.” Astoundingly, the process of putting human beings to death is less regulated than similar processes for animals.

On top of the inexperienced administrators, the dosages for lethal injection are not calculated based on body weight or any other individual factor as in nearly every other medical practice, but rather are given according to flat volumes. Because vastly different individuals may receive the same dosage, it is very possible that the anesthetic doesn’t take effect at all. When combined with the paralytic agent, patients may become unable to move but still fully aware of their senses. This seems to be the experience of Florida inmate Angel Diaz, who was observed to still have partial control of facial facilities 24 minutes into his execution.

While this study hopefully marks the first of many similar evaluations, access to relevant data is extremely limited: less than 10% of the 891 lethal injections carried out between 1984–2007 were available to these researchers. With nearly every scientific study using data as a foundation to make conclusions, how can we expect these techniques to be further developed when this data isn’t available?

The inefficacy of the “accepted” lethal injection procedures are a grim testament to the lack of sufficient research on the subject. The chemical brew was thrown together on the whim of an unqualified council, and the inconsistency of the implementation further undermines any semblance of credibility.
