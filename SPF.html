Motivation

Granular materials comprise a large group of systems that are made of lots of individual, athermal grains. Their dynamics are strongly influenced by dissipative particle-particle interactions, of which friction is a very important example.

Even though the influence of friction extends far beyond granular materials, we still have yet to put forth a complete description of its mechanism -- or mechanisms. Such an understanding would be fantastically important in both academic and industrial contexts, especially when it comes to understanding frictional failure.

The simplest, and most widely known, model of friction is the one-parameter Coulomb law1: when the ratio of tangential to normal force acting at a contact exceeds a certain value, the contact yields, or fails.

While this simple model generally works when you zoom out (literally or metaphorically) on a system, there are other types of friction that are only captured by more complex models. For example, when objects have rotational freedom, we have to think about rolling friction2, and if we don't restrict ourselves to solid systems, we must consider fluid friction3.

Our work looks to shed some light on the difference between sliding and rolling friction in a granular system, especially as particles locally rearrange, undergoing constant build up and release of interparticle stresses.

The Experiment

In our 2D system, photoelastic disks are driven through a constrictive gap of set width, during which we observe the build up and release of stress on the particles.

This experiment sits on an air table, which minimizes the effects of basal friction by floating the particles just above the bottom panel.

We make measurements of the interparticle forces using the photoelastic response of the particles4: these materials have stress-dependent optical properties, leading to areas with higher forces appearing brighter.

We also track the linear and rotational positions of the particles using convolutional techniques, which allows us to calculate linear and angular velocities throughout the rearrangement event.

For more information about all of these methods, check out "The Analysis".

The Analysis

When describing our granular system, there are three fundamental things we want to know:

  1. How are the particles moving?
  2. How are the particles rotating?
  3. What forces are acting on the particles?

We can source all three of these descriptors from images taken of our system; we'll go through how we understand and quantify each one in the sections below!

Linear Motion

To understand how a particle is moving, we need to know where the particle is located at any given time. We choose our particles to be circles (or rather, disks) which makes it very easy to track their centers as the system evolves.

We use a convolutional tracking technique5, which quantifies how likely it is that a circle is centered at each point. This works extraordinarily well, no doubt in part due to the fact that we use very high resolution images, to the point that we can track the particles even to sub-pixel accuracy!

Rotational Motion

Tracking the rotational motion of particles is a little harder than linear motion, since our particles are circularly symmetric. To track rotations, we need some artifact of the particles that does not have rotational symmetry, a candidate for which is not immediately obvious, at least to the human eye.

The solution here lies in something that might otherwise be considered noise in the images: the speckle pattern on the bottom of each particle. This pattern, an artifact of the method by which the particles are created, is not only unique, but anisotropic.

We can identify the relative rotation between any two frames by rotating one image until the two speckle patterns line up with each other. You might expect that this would be a rather weak signal, scarcely better than the minor differences in lighting across the system, but in fact it is so strong we can easily track the rotional motion of all of our particles!

Contact Forces

Lastly, we would like to know what forces are acting on each particle, so we can differentiate normal from tangential components, calculate coefficients of friction from the Coulomb model, and identify when and where we should look for slips.

The key here is to make our particles from materials that demonstrate photoelasticity6, or stress-dependent optical properties. Given the right lighting, this visually distinguishes regions of high force from those with low forces, producing patterns like the one below.

The hard part comes from turning these images into force magnitudes, positions, and contact angles, which we need to make any quantitative observations. We build on previous methods4 that make use of an analytic description of these photoelastic responses and non-linear optimization techniques to achieve this. In particular, our developments make the technique much more sensitive to small tangential forces, which can otherwise be very difficult to detect in the shadows of the larger, normal components.
