Predator-Prey Interactions of Spiders

Jack Featherstone (jack.featherstone@oist.jp) and Mahesh Bandi

Nonlinear and Non-equilibrium Physics Unit, OIST Graduate University
OIST-Kyudai Symposium: Bio-Inspired Wonders and Energy Innovations

Motivation

Spiderwebs are fantastically versatile and complex dwellings, hunting tools, and defensive mechanisms, with structures that vary from the familiar 2D circular pattern of an orb weaver to seemingly-random 3D tangles built by cobweb spiders.

Beyond this simple dichotomous classification, web structure -- and thus function -- varies drastically depending on the arachnid's biology, environmental conditions, and even day-to-day factors like hunger. We are interested in investigating these relationships, especially as they apply to prey capture efficacy, for spiders of the Latrodectus genus, and, in particular, the Australian red back spider (Latrodectus hasseltii).

Unlike most other Theridiid, or cobweb, spiders, members of the Latrodectus genus focus their prey capture efforts on ground-based insects, through the use of vertically placed gumfooted threads1. By placing glue at the bottom of these threads, passerby insects that blunder into them become stuck, and the spider can retrieve them at its convenience (for example, see [2]).

But how does the spider decide where to place these gumfooted traps? Does its strategy (if it is even appropriate to reference such a thing) vary based on the available prey for the spider, or the geometry of its environment?

Considering the "two-body problem" of the spider and the pedestrian prey, how long does it typically take for the insect to blunder into the trap? Can this distribution be recreated by modelling the prey as some type of two-dimensional walk through a field of random traps?

Click any of the sections below to explore how we are looking to answer some of these questions!

Web Scanning

If we want to test the spider's choice of trap placement as we did in the previous section, we ought to be able to quantify the web woven by these spiders. For inherently two-dimensional webs as woven by an orb-weaver, this can be easily done by taking a planar snapshot (with an appropriately chosen plane of course), as in the (infamous) study of spider web construction under the influence of various drugs3.

The three-dimensional case is much more challenging. To address this, we implement a scanning method4 that merges planar slices of a web created by scattering a laser sheet off the web strands.

A scan of a sample web (unknown species, caught around Okinawa) is shown below.

Applied to our Latrodectus spiders, this can give us the spatial distribution of traps.

But of course scanning only a single web doesn't tell us much about the trends and patterns in how the traps are made. For that, we have to scan enough to be able to look at statistics.

First Passage Simulations

How can we quantify the question of whether a configuration of traps is "effective" or not?

When we say effective here, we mean that if there is an agent walking around (say an ant or beetle), it will become trapped with a high probability and/or in a short period of time. So to quantify this, we should simulate what happens when such an agent walks around in a given field of traps!

To demonstrate, let's consider two different ways to arrange our traps: in a crystalline (square lattice) pattern, or distributed completely randomly (but uniformly).

Let's first consider just one part of the proposed quantification above: which of these two configurations will lead to capture faster (on average)?

We will test this by placing a random walker inside of our trapped region (starting at some random point), and then taking steps until we hit a trap. Rigorously, we will define both our traps and walker to have some finite but small size, and we will take steps with a uniform angle distribution (same probability to move in any direction) and a Guassian step size distribution (no huge steps allowed).

We perform this trapping simulation for a large number of runs (~105), and then calculate the histogram of how long it takes for the walker to get trapped for both configurations.

First off, we immediately notice that there is a difference between the two curves, even though the total number of traps remains the same in both cases! So indeed there are better and worse ways to place our traps. On top of this, both distributions are exponential (a straight line in semi-log); this behavior is typical of trapping phenomena with lots of traps, or generally of first passage phenomena of not-so-rare events.

These distributions should also depend on the parameters for the walker; if it makes bigger steps, or chooses its directions non-randomly, or has a larger size, we expect to see different results.

So we have the language to describe the effectiveness of a set of traps, now, instead of looking at arbitrarily chosen distributions, we want to see how the spider makes her traps.
